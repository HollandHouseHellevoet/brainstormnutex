The Mispricing

Why the Stock Is Cheap

Three reasons, in order of importance.

01: IDR Concentration

Between 50 and 60 percent of claims go through Independent Dispute Resolution under the No Surprises Act. Win rate above 85 percent. Collection rate above 85 percent on wins. Arbitration costs were $138.3M in 2025, approximately 26 percent of arbitration-related revenue.

The Q4 2025 earnings included a one-time $55M revenue reduction from 18,950 claims deemed ineligible. The market priced that as permanent impairment. It was a cumulative cleanup covering 18 months. The market's core concern is regulatory fragility and reputational exposure to HaloMD-related practices, not the operating results themselves.

02: Legacy Governance Overhang

The company restated financials. Two reverse stock splits totaling 150-to-1 were executed in 2024 to avoid NASDAQ delisting. A class action is pending. The 2025 10-K discloses that previously reported material weaknesses in internal controls were remediated as of December 31, 2025. Grant Thornton issued an unqualified opinion on both the financial statements and internal controls. The formal controls are clean. The market has not credited this. Institutional ownership remains at 5.3 percent.

03: Misclassification

The market prices NUTX as a healthcare operator. Healthcare operators trade at 1x to 3x revenue and 12x to 20x earnings. NUTX trades at 0.62x EV/Revenue and 9.6x trailing P/E. The discount is governance-driven, not operating-driven. Net margin is 8.1 percent, in line with or above peers.

The Model

How the Model Works

Five interlocking layers. Each one generates standalone economics. Each one makes the others more defensible.

01 Self-Funded Health Plan: The Network Builder
▾

Most health plans are built for carriers. This one is built for physicians and employers, and the distinction is not cosmetic.

Every practice, ASC, and physician-owned hospital that enrolls becomes two things at once: a participant in the plan and a credentialed facility inside the plan's network. They are not joining someone else's infrastructure. They are building their own. The network grows because physicians join. Physicians join because the network generates volume. Each new participant makes the collective more valuable to every participant already inside it.

For plan members, care inside the network carries no copay, no deductible, and no co-insurance. Zero. Rational people choose free. Benefit design does the work that sales teams spend millions trying to do. Volume routes itself to collective physicians without a single additional administrative step.

For self-funded employers, the value is structural, not marginal. They are not getting a discount off a carrier's inflated fee schedule. They are contracting directly with a physician-controlled network at rates set by the physicians themselves, adjudicated through UMR, Aetna, or Meritain's ASO rails. The carrier is no longer the economic gatekeeper. The employer pays less. The physician collects more. The spread that used to belong to the carrier stays inside the system.

That direct contracting arm is what makes this a two-sided marketplace. On one side: thousands of independent physicians and facilities who gain a distribution engine, a known fee schedule, and a predictable payment timeline without surrendering ownership or governance. On the other: self-funded employers who gain access to a credentialed network at 30 to 40 percent below carrier pricing, with full ASO adjudication and no disruption to existing HR infrastructure.

As collective enrollment grows, the Tier 1 network expands across all 12 states. More employers buy in. More premium flows in. More surplus accumulates. Surplus is returned to participants or reinvested in lower rates, which attracts more employers. The flywheel does not require acquisition, merger, or governance change. It requires participation. And participation is voluntary, because the economics make the decision obvious.

02 Captive Insurance: The Float Engine
▾

Premium collected from member physicians, hospitals, and their employees is held as reserves. Claims are paid from the reserve. Surplus remains as equity. Float is investable from day one. This is the mechanism Berkshire Hathaway used through GEICO and General Re.

04 Adjacent Builds: The Network Density Play
▾

There are approximately 184 physician-owned hospitals remaining in the US. ~170 are surgical. They have ORs, surgeons, and procedure volume. What they lack is a front door: no ER, no 24/7, no medical inpatient beds. A Nutex micro hospital is the inverse. All front door.

Place a micro hospital next to a surgical POH and you get a mini medical campus. The micro hospital feeds surgical cases. The POH provides post-op safety net. Neither can replicate what the other has. Both become more valuable.

170 pairings transform the collective from 27 locations to 197 campus nodes. The 2010 ACA moratorium froze POH expansion. These facilities have been legally prohibited from growing for over a decade. A Nutex micro hospital next door is the growth they cannot build themselves.

80+ percent of POHs have fully paid-off real estate. Adjacent land is often available. The POH's own unencumbered assets can participate in funding the adjacent build. Capital sequencing: 30-40 priority builds over 60 months at $20M each = $600M to $800M, funded by float, equity, and POH real estate participation.

▾

Potential Paths

How This Could Develop

There is no contractual relationship between MedMerge and Nutex Health at this time. The information Tom Vo requested was delivered on April 5. What follows is how a relationship could develop if there is mutual interest.

The lowest-friction entry point is a vendor relationship: MedMerge deploys the health plan, payroll, and HR infrastructure across Nutex facilities as a contracted service. This proves the model at the facility level, builds relationships with operating partners, and generates the data a deeper conversation requires.

If that produces results, the conversation naturally expands. Collective enrollment across Nutex's 12 states, captive deployment, and direct-to-employer contracting become the next layer. At that point MedMerge is not a vendor. It is the financial infrastructure the platform cannot operate without.

The third path follows from the second. The stock is down 98.3 percent from its all-time high on a split-adjusted basis. Institutional ownership sits at 5.3 percent. If MedMerge is embedded in the operations and the operating partners trust the model, the leadership conversation happens on its own timeline and at the right moment.

None of these paths are in conflict. Each one makes the next more likely.

What I Need From You

Break It

Here are seven questions to consider.

  • Is the health plan scalable across independent physicians, or does it only work in controlled settings? The pilot is one hospital. The thesis requires thousands of practices. What breaks in the middle?
  • Can captive economics hold with a heterogeneous risk pool? The quality gate is designed to manage this, but as enrollment scales, selection pressure weakens. How do you think about this?
  • Does the Fiserv analogy hold? Fiserv's moat is switching cost and regulatory complexity. Is the same true for a health plan and captive structure, or can a physician leave the collective without friction?
  • What is the incumbent response? If this works, UnitedHealth, Elevance, and the large systems see it. What do they do? Can they replicate it, or does their employed-physician model prevent them from offering physician-owned economics?
  • Is the adjacency play worth the capital? 30-40 builds at $20M each is $600-800M. Is the network density effect worth that deployment, or is collective enrollment alone sufficient to fill the Tier 1 network?
  • What would you need to see in the next 12 months before you would commit capital, advisory time, or a board seat? Be specific. I can deliver metrics. I need to know which ones matter to you.
